    Sesquilinear Forms:

    Consider a filed \(\mathbb{K}\) either the field \(\mathbb{C}\) of complex numbers or the field \(\mathbb{R}\) of real numbers. Let \(H\) be a vector space over \(\mathbb{K}\).

    • Sesquilinear form (A mapping \(s : H \times H \to \mathbb{K}\))
      • \[ s(f,ag+bh) = as(f,g) + bs(f,h) \] \[ s(af+bg,h) = a^*s(f,h) + b^*s(g,h) \]

      \(\forall f,g,h \in H\) and \(\forall a,b \in \mathbb{K}\). Where \(a^*\) and \(b^*\) denote the complex conjugate of \(a\) and \(b\), respectively.

    Bilinear Form:

    A bilinear form on \(H\) is given by:

    \[ s(f,ag + bh) = as(f,g) + bs(f,h) \] \[ s(af + bg,h) = as(f,h) + bs(g,h) \]

    *In particular, every sesquilinear form on a real vector space is a bilinear form.

    Scalar Product:

    A positive sesquilinear form on \(H\) is called a scalar product on \(H\). it will be denoted by \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle\). The mapping \(s : H \times H \to \mathbb{R}\) is a scalar product if, and only if \(\forall f,g,h \in H\) and \(a \in \mathbb{K}\):

    • \(s(f,g,h) = s(f,g) + s(f,h)\);
    • \(s(f,ag) = as(f,g)\)
    • \(s(f,g) = s(g,f)^*\)
    • \(s(f,f) \geq 0\)
    • \(s(f,f) > 0\) if \(f \neq 0\)

    *A semi-scalar product is a nonegative sesquilinear form.

    Norm:

    A mapping \(p : H \to \mathbb{R}\) is called a norm (denoted by \(||\cdot||\)) on \(H\) if, and only if, \(\forall f,g \in H\) and \(\forall a \in \mathbb{K}\):

    • \(p(f) \geq 0\);
    • \(p(af) = |a|p(f)\);
    • \(p(f + g) \leq p(f) + p(g)\) (triangle inequality);
    • \(p(f) > 0\) provided \(f \neq 0\)

    *The mapping will be called a seminorm on \(H\) if it satisfies (i-iii).

    Pre-Hilbert and Norm Spaces:

    If \(H\) is a vector space \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle\) is a scalar product on \(H\), then we call the pair \(H, \langle \cdot, \cdot, \rangle\) a pre-Hilbert space.

    If \(||\cdot||\) is a norm on \(H\), then we call the pair \(H, ||\cdot||\) a normed space.

    Cauchy sequence:

    A sequence \((f_n)\) in a normed space \(H\) is called a Cauchy sequence if, for every \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists an \(n_0 \in \mathbb{N}\) such that \(||f_n - f_m|| \leq \varepsilon\). With:

    • if \((f_n)\) is a Cauchy sequence, then the sequence \(||f_n||\) is convergent (thus it is bounded);
    • if \(H,\langle \dot , \cdot \rangle\) is a pre-Hilbert space and \((f_n), (g,n)\) are Cauchy sequences, then the sequence \(\langle f_n, g_n \rangle\) is convergent.

    Banach and Hilbert Spaces:

    A complete normed space is called a Banach space \((\mathbb{B})\).

    A complete pre-Hilbert space is called a Hilbert space \((\mathbb{H})\).

    A normed space \((H,||\cdot||)\) is said to be complete if every Cauchy sequence is convergent.

    Banach and Hilbert Spaces:

    For \(\mathbb{X}\) and \(\mathbb{Y}\) complex Banach spaces, set \(\mathbb{B}(\mathbb{X},\mathbb{Y})\) the Banach space of all bounded linear operators of \(\mathbb{X}\) and \(\mathbb{Y}\). And for simplicity \(\mathbb{B}(\mathbb{X}) \triangleq \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{X},\mathbb{X})\).

    Two norms \(||\cdot||\) in a Banach space \(\mathbb{X}\) are equivalent if for some \(c_1 > 0\), \(c_2 > 0\) and \(\forall x \in \mathbb{X}\):

    \[ ||x||_1 \leq c_2||x||_2 \quad, ||x||_2 \leq c_1||x||_1 \]

    Linear Operators:

    A linear operator \(T\) from \(H_1\) into \(H_2\) is, by definition, a linear mapping of a subsbace \(D(T)\) of \(H_1\) into \(H_2\). Where the subspace \(D(T)\) is called the domain of \(T\).

    The image \(R(T) - T(D(T)) = \{T_f : f \in D(T)\}\) is called the range of \(T\).

    If \(H_1 = H_2 = H\), the \(T\) is called an operator on \(H\) and a linear operator from \(H into \mathbb{K}\) is called a linear functional.

    An operator is injective iff \(T_f = 0\) implies \(f = 0\).

    An operator is said to be bijective if \(T\) is injective and \(R(T) = H_2\).

    Bounded Operators:

    An operator \(T\) from \(H_1\) into \(H_2\) is said to be bounded if there exists a \(C \geq 0\) such that \(||T_f|| \leq C||f||\) \(\forall f \in D(T)\). Any such \(C\) is called a bound of \(T\).

    Let's call the set of the bounded operators from \(H_1\) into \(H_2\) as \(B(H_1,H_2)\). Then \(B(H) \triangleq B(H,H)\).

    Open and Closed subsets:

    Let \(H\) be a normed space. A subset \(A\) of \(H\) is said to be open if for each \(f \in A\) there exists a \(\varepsilon > 0\) such that:

    \[ K(f,\varepsilon) = \{g \in H : ||g - f|| < \varepsilon\} \]

    lies in \(A\).

    A subset \(A\) of \(H\) is said to be closed if \(CA = H / A\), the complement of \(A\) is open.

    Closed and Closable Operators:

    Let \(T\) be an operator from \(H_1\) into \(H_2\). The graph of \(T\) is the subset of \(H_1 \times H_2\), where \(H_1 \times H_2\) can be considered a Hilbert space \((H_1 \times H_2 = H_1 \oplus H_2)\):

    \[ G(T) = \{(f, T_f) : f \in D(T)\} \]

    An operator is said to be closed if its graph \(G(T) \in H_1 \times H_2\) is closed.

    An operator is said to be closable if \(G(T)\) is a graph.

    Resolvent Set:

    Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space over \(\mathbb{K}\) and let \(T\) be an operator on \(H\). The number \(z \in \mathbb{K}\) is called an eigenvalue of \(T\) if there exists an \(f \in D(T)\), \(f \neq 0\) such that \(T_f = zf\).

    The element \(f\) is called an eigenelement or eigenvector of \(T\) belonging to the eigenvalue \(z\). If \(z\) is not an eigenvalue, then the operator \(R(z,T) = (zI - T)^{-1}\) is called the resolvent of \(T\) at the point \(z\) and it's well defined.

    Resolvent Set:

    Then the resolvent set of \(T\) is given by:

    \[ p(T) = \{z \in \mathbb{K} : z - T \text{ is injective, and } R(z,T) \in B(H)\} \]

    And for a closed operator \(T\) on \(H\), we have:

    \[ p(T) = \{z \in \mathbb{K} : z - T \text{ is bijective.}\} \]

    Spectrum:

    The spectrum of \(T\) is given by:

    \[ \sigma(T) = \mathbb{K} / p(T) = C_k p(T) \]

    Where the set \(\sigma_p(T)\) of all the eigenvalues of \(T\) is contained in \(\sigma(T)\). The set \(\sigma_p(T)\) is called the point-spectrum of \(T\).

    Von Neumann Series:

    If \(T\) is a closed operator on the Hilbert space \(H\), then \(p(T)\) is open, consequently \(\sigma(T)\) is closed. More precisely, if \(z_0 \in p(T)\), then \(z \in p(T) \quad \forall z \in \mathbb{K}\) such that \(|z-z_0| < ||R(z_0,T)||^{-1}\), and for these \(z\) we have:

    \[ R(z,T) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}{(z_0 - z)^n R(z_0,T)^{n-1}} \]

    Von Neumann Series:

    If \(T \in B(H)\), then we have \(\{z \in \mathbb{K} : |z| > ||T||\} \subset p(T)\); the spectrum \(\sigma(T)\) is compact. Furthermore:

    \[ R(z,T) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}{z^{-n-1} T^n} \]

    This series is called the von Neumann series.

    Spectral Radius:

    Assume that \(H\) is a Hilbert space and \(T \in B(H)\), the spectral radius of \(T\) is \(r(T) = \lim \sup ||T^n||^{1/n}\). Then, we have:

    • \(r(T) \leq ||T^m||^{1/m} \quad \forall m \in \mathbb{N}\) and thus \(r(T) = \lim ||T^n||^{1/n}\);
    • \(r(T) \leq ||T||\) and \(\sigma(T) \subset \{z \in \mathbb{K} : |z| \leq r(T)\} \quad \forall z \in \mathbb{K}\) such that \(|z| > r(T)\), the operator \(R(z,T)\) is given by the Neumann series.
    • If \(H\) is complex, then \(\sigma(T)\) is non-empty and there exists a \(z \in \sigma(T)\) such that \(|z| = r(T)\), i.e., we have \(r(T) = \{|z|:z \in \sigma(T)\}\).
    • The previous statement also holds for any self-adjoint \(T\) in a real Hilbert space.

    Trace Operator:

    Denote by \(\lambda_i(P), i=1,\ldots,N\) the eigenvalues of a matrix \(P \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C})^n\).

    Recall that the trace operator \(tr(\cdot) : \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n) \to \mathbb{C}\) is a linear functional with the following properties:

    • \(\tr(KL) = \tr(LK)\);
    • For any \(M, P \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n)\) with \(M \geq 0\), \(P > 0\):
    \[ \left(\min_{i=1,\ldots,n} \lambda_i(P)\right)\tr(M) \leq \tr(MP) \leq \left(\max_{i=1,\ldots,n} \lambda_i(P)\right)tr(M) \]

    Convenient Space:

    A convenient space to be used is the one defined as \(\mathbb{H}^{n,m}\), which is the linear space made up of all \(N\)-sequences of complex matrix \(V = (V_1,\ldots,V_N)\) with \(V_i \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n, \mathbb{C}^m), i \in \mathbb{N}\). For simplicity \(\mathbb{H}^n \triangleq \mathbb{H}^{n,n}\).

    In this finite dimensional space \(\mathbb{H}^{n,m}\), we define the following equivalent norms:

    \[ ||V||_1 \triangleq \sum_{i=1}^{N}{||V_i||}, \quad ||V||_2 \triangleq \left(\sum_{i=1}^{N}{\tr(V_i^*V_i)}\right)^{1/2} \]

    Convenient Space:

    \[ ||V||_{max} \triangleq \max \{||V_i||, \quad i \in \mathbb{N}\} \]

    *We shall omit the subscripts whenever the definition of a specific norm does not affect the result being considered.

    Convenient Space:

    It is easy to verify that \(\mathbb{H}^{n,m}\) equipped with any of the previous norms is a Banach space and, in fact \((||\cdot||_2,\mathbb{H}^{n,m})\) is a Hilbert space, with the inner product given, for \(V = (V_1,\ldots,V_N)\) and \(S = (s_1,\ldots,S_N) \in \mathbb{H}^{n,m}\), by:

    \[ \langle V,S \rangle \triangleq \sum_{i=1}^{N}{\tr(V_i^*S_i)} \]

    Convenient Space:

    It is also convenient to define the equivalent induced norms for the operator \(T \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{H^n})\):

    \[ ||T||_1 \triangleq \sup_{V \in \mathbb{H}^n} \frac{||T(V)||_1}{||V||_1}, \quad ||T||_2 \triangleq \sup_{V \in \mathbb{H}^n} \frac{||T(V)||_2}{||V||_2} \]

    *We shall omit the subscripts whenever the definition of a specific norm does not affect the result being considered.

    Hermitian Matrix and operator:

    For \(V = (V_1,\ldots,V_N) \in \mathbb{H}^{n,m}\) we write \(V^* = (v_1^*,\ldots,V_N^*) \in \mathbb{H}^{m,n}\), and say that \(V \in \mathbb{H}^n\) is hermitian if \(V = V^*\). Then, set:

    \[ H^{n^*} \triangleq \{V \in \mathbb{H}^{n*}; V_i = V_i^*, i \in \mathbb{N}\} \] \[ H^{n^+} \triangleq \{V \in \mathbb{H}^{n+}; V_i \geq 0, i \in \mathbb{N}\} \]

    You can say that \(V \geq S\) if \(V-S = (V_1 - S_1, \ldots, V_N - S_n) \in \mathbb{H}^{n+}\). And that an operator \(T \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{H}^n)\) is hermitian if \(T(V) \in \mathbb{H}^{n^*}\) whenever \(V \in \mathbb{K}^{n*}\), and that it is positive if \(T(V) \in \mathbb{H}^{n+}\) whenever \(V \in \mathbb{H}^{n+}\).

    Vectorization Mappings:

    Define the operators \(\phi\) and \(\hat{\phi}\) that acts as mathematical mappings that allow the operator \(T\) to be represented in a different coordinate system, with \(V = (V_1,\ldots,V_N) \in \mathbb{H}^{n,m}\), with \(V_i = [v_i1 \quad \ldots \quad v_in] \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n,\mathbb{C}^m), v_{ij} \in \mathbb{C}^m\):

    \[ \phi(V_i) \triangleq \begin{bmatrix} v_{i1}\\ \vdots\\ v_{in} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{C}^{mn} \qquad \hat{\phi}(V_i) \triangleq \begin{bmatrix} \phi(v_{i1})\\ \vdots\\ \phi(v_{in}) \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{C}^{Nmn} \]

    Kronecker Product:

    The Kronecker product of \(L,K \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n)\) is denoted by \(L \otimes K\) and is a \(\mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n)\) matrix defined by:

    \[ L \otimes K = \begin{bmatrix} l_{1,1}K & l_{1,2}K & \ldots & l_{1,n}K\\ l_{2,1}K & l_{2,2}K & \ldots & l_{2,n}K\\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \cdots\\ l_{n,1}K & l_{n,2}K & \ldots & l_{n,n}K\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

    Kronecker Product:

    And the following properties holds:

    \[ (L \otimes K)^* = L^* \otimes K^* \] \[ \phi(LKH) = (H' \otimes L)\phi(K), H \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n) \]

    Homeomorphic Spaces:

    A mapping \(G : (X,d_1) \to (Y,d_2)\) is saitd to be a homeomorphism if \(G\) is invertible and \(G,G^{-1}\) are continuous.

    Two metric spaces \((X,d_1)\) and \((Y,d2)\) are said to be homeomorphic if there exists a homeomorphism mapping one of them onto the other.

    It is easy to verify, through the mapping \(\hat{\phi}\), that the spaces \(\mathbb{H}^{n,m}\) and \(\mathbb{C}^{Nmn}\) are uniformly homeomorphic and that any operator \(Z \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{H}^{n,m})\) can be represented in \(\mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^{Nmn})\) through the mapping \(\hat{\phi}\). We shall denote this operator by \(\hat{\phi}[Z]\). And we must have:

    \[ r_{\sigma}(Z) = r_{\sigma}[\hat{\phi}[Z]] \]

    Orthogonal Matrix:

    If \(W \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n)^{+}\) then there exists a unique \(W^{1/2} \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^{+})\) such that \(W = (W^{1/2})^2\). The absolute value of \(W \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n)\), denoted by \(|W|\), is defined as \(|W| \triangleq (W^*W)^{1/2}\).

    Then, there exists an orthogonal matrix \(U \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C})^n\) (that is \(U^{-1} = U\)), such that:

    \[ W = U |W| \quad \text{or} |W| = U^{-1}W = U^*W \] \[ ||W|| = |\mid |W| \mid| \]

    Self-adjoint:

    For any \(W \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n)\) there exists \(W^j, j = 1,2,3,4\) such that \(W^j \geq 0\) and \(||W^j|| \leq ||W||\) for \(j = 1,2,3,4\) and \(W = (W^1 - W^2) + \sqrt{-1}(W^3 - W^4)\). We can write:

    \[ W = V^{1} + \sqrt{-1}V^2 \]

    where:

    \[ V^1 = \frac{1}{2}(W^* + W) \qquad V^2 = \frac{\sqrt{-1}}{2}(W^* - W) \]

    Self-adjoint:

    Since \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) are self-adjoint (that is, \(Vî = V^{i*}, i = 1,2\)), and every self-adjoint element in \(\mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n)\) can be decomposed into positive and negative parts, we have that there exist a \(W^i \in \mathbb{B}(\mathbb{C}^n), i = 1,2,3,4\), such that:

    \[ V^1 = W^1 - W^2 \qquad V^2 = W^3 - W^4 \]

    Therefore for any \(S = (S_1,\ldots,S_N) \in \mathbb{H}^n\), we can find \(S^j \in \mathbb{H}^{n^+}, j = 1,2,3,4\) such that \(||S^j||_1 \leq ||S||_1\) and:

    \[ S = (S^1 - S^2) + \sqrt{-1}(S^3 - S^4) \]
